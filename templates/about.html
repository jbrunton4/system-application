Project Purpose

This project is intended to be a simple solution to present and organise information about DID/OSDD systems and their alters. (See What Is DID/OSDD? ).

While not the original intended purpose, as with later-discussed solutions, this application can be used for organising other information, such as that of original characters (OCs).

What Is DID/OSDD?

Both DID and OSDD are trauma response disorders more commonly known by the more outdated and stigmatised term, Multiple Personality Disorder (MPD). MPD is a term first introduced in the DSM-3 in 1980, and was later reclassified into DID and OSDD.

In both DID and OSDD, multiple "alters" with different personalities, memories, and experiences are present inside the same body. One or more alters may control the body at any time. Note that while "alter" is arguably the most common term, different systems may prefer different terms.

A group of alters together in a body is called a system. In other words, a system describes a body with DID/OSDD and the alters therein. Some people might "have", "are", or "are part of" a system.

DID (Dissociative Identity Disorder) is closest to the common idea of MPD, with alters usually unable to remember what happened while they were not "in front" (controlling the body), though as with all systems, this will vary on a case-by-case basis.

OSDD (Other Specified Dissociative Disorder, formerly DDNOS Dissociative Disorder Not Otherwise Specified) typically involves less amnesia than DID. There are also 4 separate types:

  1. Sub-threshold DID, described as "Partial DID"
  2. Caused by "prolonged and intense coercive persuasion"
  3. Acute dissociative reactions to stressful events
  4. Loss of awareness of surroundings

These descriptions are very limited and do not at all characterise the full extent of what DID and OSDD entail. It is encouraged to do your own research, and always accept that different systems can have vastly different experiences that may seem impossible to those without DID/OSDD.

What Is A Subsystem?

To understand the concept of a subsystem, it is first essential to understand the concept of a headspace or innerworld.

A headspace, sometimes also called an innerworld or inner world, is a dream-like space inside the brain where alters usually have their own forms. These forms can vary wildly in size, shape, species, and more. Headspaces usually also contain objects, buildings, and other things.

Usually, an area of headspace represents "front", where alters can go to experience and control the body. Note that some systems may instead have an "object front", which connects alters to front while they are touching it, and some may have both or neither.

Alters' unique bodies in headspace are also subject to developing their own dissociative disorders. This can mean that an alter in a system can develop their own subsystem with their own headspace, known as a "subsystem", and share their headspace body with subsystem alters.

A subsystem, therefore, is in its most fundamental sense, a system within a system.

Inspiring Solutions

For many systems, the preferred app for communicating is Discord . While Discord has many features that make it appealing to a wide variety of users, a particularly helpful feature for systems is the Webhooks feature, which allows for an activity commonly known as "proxying".

Discord Bots can send requests to Webhooks, which will in turn send a message to a specified channel. This is different from users and bots sending regular messages in a few key ways. Importantly for proxying, one such difference is that on top of message content, display names and profile pictures for messages sent via webhook are on a per-message basis.

In practice, this means that with the help of a bot, a user can send messages to servers under a different name and avatar from that of their account or server profile, typically by putting text before, after, or around their message content. This is easiest to see in action:

A demonstration of Discord Proxying using my account and a proxy for one of my D&D characters.

The two most common proxying solutions for systems, role-players and more are TupperBox and PluralKit . TupperBox is more beginner-friendly, while PluralKit boasts a more diverse set of features.

Both TupperBox and PluralKit have web dashboards that can be used to view and edit limited information regarding proxies. Discord users can also request information from the bots, but the text commands are difficult to remember and format. As such, it is often difficult to easily represent information regarding different proxies. This solution aims to combat the problem by providing a simple space to store and search information regarding such settings.
